Summary

There are two clear peaks in daily reported cases, one around January 2021 and a much larger peak around November 2021. This data does not account for the first wave in March 2020, because testing was not widely available.

Deaths here are defined as a death of an individual who received a positive COVID-19 within 28 days of death. We have more confidence in the quality of this data because it is harder to miss a death than an asymptomatic case. The day with the most deaths was {{ data_dict["dayOfMaxDeaths"] }}. Half of all COVID-19 related deaths to date had occurred by 22/01/2022.

Here, a day’s mortality is defined as the day’s daily deaths divided by the day’s daily cases. Since early reported cases are likely to be an underestimate of actual cases, the high early mortality is probably an overestimate. The average mortality to date is {{ data_dict["fullMortality"] }}. However, if we split the data at 200 days in we see an early mortality of {{ data_dict["earlyMortality"] }}, and a later mortality of just {{ data_dict["lateMortality"] }}. It is unclear how much of the high early mortality can be attributed to daily case underestimation, and how much is due to real causes. The value {{ data_dict["lateMortality"] }} can be taken as a reasonable mortality estimate.
